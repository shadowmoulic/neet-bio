Taking in O₂ and expelling CO₂ — respiratory organs, the mechanism of breathing, lung volumes & capacities, how gases are exchanged and transported, and respiratory disorders.

Chapter Content: Study Notes MCQ Practice Flashcards

4.1 Respiratory Organs

Breathing exchanges atmospheric O₂ for the CO₂ made by cells, in two phases — inspiration and expiration. Animals respire by different structures depending on habitat and body plan:

  • Body surface — sponges, coelenterates, flatworms.
  • Moist skin / cuticle — earthworms (cutaneous).
  • Tracheal tubes — insects.
  • Gills (branchial) — fishes, prawns.
  • Lungs (pulmonary) — terrestrial vertebrates.

Human air passage:

Nostrils→Nasal chamber→Pharynx→Larynx→Trachea→Bronchi → bronchioles→Alveoli
Conducting part (to terminal bronchioles) cleans, warms & humidifies air; alveoli + ducts are the exchange part.
  • The larynx (sound box) helps produce sound; the epiglottis guards the glottis during swallowing.
  • The trachea and bronchi have incomplete cartilage rings to stop collapse; the trachea divides at about the 5th thoracic vertebra.
  • Lungs sit in the thoracic chamber, covered by a double-walled pleura with lubricating pleural fluid.

4.2 Mechanism of Breathing & Lung Volumes

Air movement follows Boyle's law (pressure ∝ 1/volume at constant temperature):

InspirationExpiration (normal)
DiaphragmContracts, flattensRelaxes, domes up
External intercostalsContract; ribs & sternum up/outRelax
Thoracic & lung volumeIncreaseDecrease
Intra-pulmonary pressureBelow atmospheric → air inAbove atmospheric → air out (passive)

A healthy human breathes 12–16 times/min. Volumes are measured with a spirometer.

4.3 Exchange of Gases

Gas exchange at the alveoli (and at tissues) happens by simple diffusion, driven by partial-pressure gradients. The diffusion membrane has three thin layers — alveolar squamous epithelium, capillary endothelium and the basement substance between (~0.2 mm).

Partial pressure (mm Hg)O₂CO₂
Atmospheric air1590.3
Alveoli10440
Oxygenated blood9540
Tissues / deoxygenated blood4045

O₂ diffuses alveoli → blood → tissues; CO₂ diffuses tissues → blood → alveoli. CO₂ is ~20–25× more soluble than O₂, so it diffuses readily despite a smaller gradient.

4.4 Transport of Gases

Oxygen: ~97% is carried as oxyhaemoglobin (each haemoglobin binds up to 4 O₂); ~3% dissolves in plasma. Binding is favoured by high pO₂, low pCO₂, low H⁺ and low temperature (in the lungs).

The oxygen-dissociation curve is sigmoid. In tissues (low pO₂, high pCO₂, high H⁺, higher temperature) it shifts right and O₂ is unloaded — the Bohr effect.

Carbon dioxide is transported three ways:

FormShare
Bicarbonate ions (HCO₃⁻)~70%
Carbamino-haemoglobin~20–25%
Dissolved in plasma~7%
CO₂ + H₂O ⇌(carbonic anhydrase) H₂CO₃ ⇌ HCO₃⁻ + H⁺

Every 100 mL of oxygenated blood delivers ~5 mL O₂ to tissues; every 100 mL of deoxygenated blood carries ~4 mL CO₂ to the alveoli.

4.5 Regulation of Respiration

The body precisely controls breathing through centres in the brain:

  • Respiratory rhythm centre (medulla oblongata) — sets the basic rhythm.
  • Pneumotaxic centre (pons) — can reduce the duration of inspiration, altering the rate.
  • Chemosensitive area (near the rhythm centre) — highly sensitive to CO₂ and H⁺; a rise signals adjustments to expel CO₂. O₂ has little direct role.

4.6 Disorders of the Respiratory System

  • Asthma: difficulty in breathing with wheezing, due to inflammation and spasm of the bronchi/bronchioles (often allergic).
  • Emphysema: alveolar walls are damaged, lowering the respiratory surface area; cigarette smoking is a major cause.
  • Occupational respiratory disorders: long dust exposure (stone-grinding, mining) inflames and scars the lungs — e.g. silicosis and asbestosis.
